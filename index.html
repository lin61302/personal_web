Building data + AI systems for social good.

Politics can feel like it’s moving faster than anyone can process. The headlines refresh before the context does. In that kind of world, AI has turned into a weirdly reliable companion: an information hub when you need grounding, and honestly, emotional support when the news won’t stop. Whether we like it or not, it’s also evolving faster than it used to, which makes learning with it, and learning to live with it, more important than ever. It won’t solve everything, but it can make it easier to stay oriented: collect the signal, check the evidence, and avoid getting pulled around by the loudest narrative.

The space and the motivation are personal. I’ve seen and learned enough, domestically and internationally, to know how tragic, draining, and frankly difficult some realities can be to sit with. So the question becomes: what if the constructive side of tech power were used well? Countering erosion and polarization, widening awareness, and lowering the friction for people who want to engage. Exposure shapes what people notice and what they care about. So the aim here isn’t “cool AI.” It’s using the tools well.

About

When politics fractures, we stay grounded—and build a better vision.

What I do

I lead multiple large-scale ML projects in political, environmental, and social science contexts to identify and track events, trends, and risks. I’m passionate about contributing to the civil world, where information can change outcomes for the better—especially at this time, when uncertainty and instability are high.

How I work

I begin with the research questions and a measurement design, then build end-to-end systems that convert messy signals into decision-ready insight. The workflow spans data collection, processing, modeling (training + validation), and public-facing presentation, so the evidence stays connected and the storyline supports action.
